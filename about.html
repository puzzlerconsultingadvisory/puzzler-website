A Note from Mark

People remember FEMA for the emergencies. I remember it for what came after.

As an emergency manager, I learned that the work that really matters isn't the response — it's helping people find their way back to a new normal. Not their old life. A new one. You can't hand someone a plan and call that help. You have to get down where they are, see what they see, and stay with them until the ground feels steady again.

There's a scene from The West Wing I think about. A guy is stuck in a hole. A friend jumps in beside him. The guy says: "What the hell are you doing? Now we're both in the hole." The friend says: "Yeah, but I've been down here before. I know the way out."

That's what I learned at FEMA. It's what Puzzler is built to do.

I've spent two decades thriving in complex, high-priority environments — and in the smaller, more meaningful engagements where systems and processes actually get modernized. Each chapter of that career taught me something I carry into Puzzler:

  • At the Joint Staff / Department of Defense, I served as an action officer engaging the highest levels of defense leadership — and learned how consequential decisions actually get made at the top.
  • At FEMA, I held what remains my coolest title: Gatekeeper to the Joint Requirements Council. I built FEMA's capability and requirements development function from the ground up — and learned that real modernization starts with asking the right questions, not buying the latest tools.
  • At the USDA's Food Safety and Inspection Service, I worked alongside the people who feed our communities and keep the food safe — and learned that mission clarity at the front line is worth more than any strategy deck.
  • At the U.S. Department of State, I now facilitate the exchange programs that enable people-to-people diplomacy across the world — and see every day what happens when strangers are given the chance to meet as humans.

What ties those chapters together is simple. Organizations don't fail because their strategy is wrong. They fail because no one stays in the hole with them long enough to get them out. Puzzler was built to be the partner that stays.

I care deeply about the people inside institutions. That's why I advocate for adoption, particularly for older children whom the system too often overlooks, and for mental health — because the strength of any organization rests on the strength of the humans inside it. This work isn't separate from Puzzler. It's the same work at a different scale.

Every engagement begins the same way: with a conversation. Tell us where you are, where you're trying to get to, and what's in the way. We'll get in the hole with you — and help you find your new normal.

— Mark

For the Record

Mark D. Williams is the founder of Puzzler Consulting & Advisory Services. He currently serves as Deputy Executive Director at the U.S. Department of State's Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs, where he oversees a staff of 130+, a budget exceeding $800 million, and a multi-billion-dollar grants portfolio.

His two-decade federal career spans senior leadership roles at the U.S. Department of Agriculture's Food Safety and Inspection Service, the Department of Homeland Security's FEMA, and the Joint Staff at the Department of Defense — shaping leadership development, workforce planning, grants operations, acquisition modernization, and organizational capacity across the federal enterprise.

He holds a B.A. from Howard University and is a vocal advocate for safe, accessible, and human-centered AI adoption across the public and nonprofit sectors.
